Session 1 · Part 2

Live Demo

Switch to browser now. Keep slides visible on a second monitor if possible. Narrate every click and every choice out loud — you're modelling the thinking process, not just the result.

The Demo

Walk through the demo live in the browser while this image is on screen: 1. Write a one-paragraph brief in Claude: what the site is for, who the audience is, what sections you want 2. Paste the brief into Google AI Studio — watch a site appear 3. Critique it openly: "What works? What got weird?" (Hero sections usually strong; copy often generic; images are placeholders) 4. Make one quick refinement in chat: "Make the hero section bolder and cut the text by half" 5. Compare the before and after — notice what changed and what didn't Say out loud what you are noticing at each step. Model the thinking: "I'm looking at the hero and the text feels generic — let me try asking it to be more specific." Keep total demo under 20 minutes. Don't polish the output — the goal is to show the loop, not produce a perfect site.
"The first output is never the final output."

Every great AI result is the product of iteration, not luck. The skill is in the back-and-forth — not the single prompt.

This is the session's mantra. Return to it whenever students get frustrated with a result. "Remember — first output, not final output." Write it on a whiteboard if you can.

Session 1 · Part 3

Your Turn to Build: Site #1

45 minutes · Work in pairs
Pair students now. One laptop per pair is fine — reduces anxiety, increases problem-solving. Assign pairs if the room seems hesitant to self-organise.

How Pairs Work

Person A — Types

  • Writes and submits the prompt
  • Makes refinements based on feedback
  • Shares screen so both can see

Person B — Watches & Critiques

  • Notes what landed well
  • Spots one thing to change
  • Suggests the next refinement prompt
Swap roles at the halfway mark (approx. 22 minutes in)
Circulate constantly during this block. Listen for "it's not working" — that is your teaching moment. Ask: "What did you want it to do? Let's write that as a prompt." Stalled students often won't raise their hand. Walk the room and check in proactively.

Your Starter Prompt

Create a landing page for [a photographer / writer / consultant / nonprofit / community organiser]. Use modern design with bold typography. Include: hero section, about, work samples with placeholder images, and a contact form. Tone: [professional / warm / bold / friendly].

Replace the [ brackets ] with your own words. Every choice changes the output.

Tell students to copy this prompt and fill in the three brackets. Remind them there is no wrong answer for topic — use a real project, a fictional persona, anything. The goal is to generate something, not to get it perfect.
"What surprised you? What got weird? What did AI get exactly right?"
Ask 2–3 pairs to share. Capture their answers — these become teaching material for the pattern recognition segment that follows. Listen for common themes: AI usually nails structure and layout; it usually misses voice, real detail, and anything specific to the person.

Session 1 · Part 4

The 3 Prompt Levers

Drawing on what the room just discovered.

Bring everyone back together. Ask 2–3 pairs to share one thing they noticed. Use their actual answers to bridge into the levers.

What You Just Discovered

What AI consistently nails

  • Layout, structure, section flow
  • Visual hierarchy
  • Getting something on screen — fast
  • Generating multiple options

What AI consistently misses

  • Your actual voice
  • Specific, real details about your work
  • Real content (not generic filler)
  • Anything that requires knowing you
Ask the room first: "What did AI get right without being told?" then "What did it miss?" Their answers will map exactly to these two columns. This slide validates what they experienced — it's not lecture, it's reflection.

The 3 Prompt Levers

01

Specificity

Vague prompts get generic results. Specific prompts get useful ones.

  • "Build a website" → generic
  • "Landing page for a Lagos-based food photographer, bold black and white" → useful
02

Tone

"Professional" and "warm and conversational" produce completely different sites.

  • Try: bold, minimal, playful, earthy, authoritative
  • Combine two: "warm but professional"
03

Structure

You can ask for exactly what you want — AI won't assume.

  • "3-column grid, no sidebar"
  • "One bold headline only, no subheading"
After showing all three, ask: "Which lever did you use least during the build?" Most will say Structure — it's the most abstract. Demo live: take the starter prompt and add all three levers to it.

Lever Demo: Before & After

Weak prompt
Make a website.
Generic. AI has nothing to work with.
Strong prompt
Build a one-page portfolio for a Lagos-based photographer. Use bold black and white with a gold accent. Include: hero image slot, 6-photo grid, bio section, WhatsApp contact link. Tone: editorial and confident.
Specific · Toned · Structured → useful output.
Ask the room: "What changed?" Accept answers before explaining. They'll identify specificity, tone, and structure on their own. That's the point — they already know this, they just need the vocabulary.

Session 2 · Part 1

Before/After Demo

Open the site from Session 1. Students should be watching — not building yet. You're modelling the creative director mindset.
"You are the creative director. AI is the production team."

Tell it what you want — clearly, specifically, with tone. It can produce more than just visuals: text, voice, music, images, code. Your job is to direct.

Walk through these 4 refinements live — show the prompt you type, then show the result each time: 1. "Make the hero section full-width with a single bold headline. Remove the subtitle." 2. "Change the colour palette to deep navy and warm white. No gradients." 3. "Rewrite the about section in first person. Make it sound like a real human, not a brochure." 4. Use Gemini TTS to generate a short spoken intro → play it for the room. The audio moment (step 4) usually surprises the room. Let it land: "You just recorded a voice intro. Without a studio." Keep total time under 20 minutes. This reframe shifts students from "user of a tool" to "director of a team." Ask: "What would you tell a production team if you were the creative director of your site right now?" Let a few students answer.

Session 2 · Part 2

Deep Iteration Workshop

90 minutes · Individual work

Work through the 7-Refinement Card on your real project. We start with Cloudinary setup.

This is the longest block. Students will refine their own site through 7 structured checkpoints. Start with Cloudinary — it's quick and unlocks the image and audio steps.

Cloudinary Setup

  1. Go to cloudinary.com — create a free account
  2. Click Upload → select any image from your laptop
  3. Once uploaded, click the image → Copy URL
  4. That URL is what you paste into your AI prompt: "Use this image: [URL]"
  5. Same process for audio files (voice intro, music track) — upload, copy URL, paste

Think of it as your creative storage locker — everything you make in this session lives there so your site can find it from anywhere.

Anticipate login issues. Have a shared backup Cloudinary account pre-loaded on a sticky note at each table. Walk through this step slowly — once students understand the upload → copy URL → paste flow, the rest of the image and audio steps become easy.

The 7-Refinement Card

# Focus Example prompt to try
1 Layout & Structure "Move the contact form to its own section at the bottom. Add a pricing section between work and contact."
2 Visual & Tone "Switch to a warm earthy palette — terracotta, cream, and dark brown. Make it feel handmade and personal."
3 Copy & Voice "Rewrite the hero headline to feel more direct and confident. Give me 3 options."
4 Add a Missing Piece "Add a FAQ section with 4 questions relevant to [my work]."
5 Images Use Gemini to generate 2–3 custom images → upload to Cloudinary → paste URL into the site
6 Voice Narration Use Gemini TTS to record a 20–30 second spoken intro → upload to Cloudinary → embed on the site
7 Background Music Use Gemini to generate a short ambient music track → upload to Cloudinary → embed on the site
Students work through these in any order. They do not have to complete all 7. Three solid refinements produce a dramatically better site than a rushed seven. Facilitator cues: • Stuck on #1 after 15 min → skip to #3 (copy is faster) • Finished all 7 early → bonus: generate a second music track in a different mood and compare • Site "broke" → treat as a teaching moment; ask AI to fix it in chat • Audio refinements (#6 and #7) are the most delightful — use them as a reward for anxious students

Stuck? Try One of These

Paste any of these into your AI chat to get unstuck:

"Explain what this code does in plain English."
"What would make this page look more professional? Give me 3 specific suggestions."
"Give me 3 alternative versions of this headline. Keep them short and direct."
"Something looks broken. Here's what I see: [describe it]. What could be causing this and how do I fix it?"
Leave this slide on the projector during the workshop block. It reduces the number of hands raised and builds students' confidence in self-directing.

Session 3 · Part 1

Final Polish

15 minutes

Pick your top 2 remaining fixes. Stay focused — this is not open-ended.

Ask each student: "What is the one thing that would make you proud to show this to someone?" That's the thing to fix. Not ten things — one.

Polish Checklist

Headline is clear and specific — a stranger would understand it in 3 seconds
At least one real image — not a placeholder
Page title and meta description set — use Claude: "Write a compelling page title and meta description for my site about [project]"
Preview on mobile — check for anything obviously broken
Read through the list together as a class. Students check off on their own. Three solid items beats a rushed six.

Session 3 · Part 2

Publishing

Walk through the Google AI Studio publishing flow together as a group.

Pre-test this end-to-end before the session. Know where the friction points are and have a fallback ready. If publishing fails for anyone, a shareable preview link or exported screenshot is a valid artifact — the work is real regardless of the URL.

Publishing Your Site

  1. In Google AI Studio, click Publish in the top-right corner
  2. Review the publishing options — select Public link
  3. Click Select a Cloud Project (you can use the shared class project)
  4. Click Publish and wait for the URL to generate
  5. Copy your live URL — paste it into the shared class document
  6. If using the shared class project, ask the instructor to make the website public
  7. Open your URL in a new tab to confirm it loads correctly

Shared class document

If publishing fails: A shareable preview link or screenshot is a valid artifact. The work is real — the URL is just delivery.
Have the shared class document open on the projector. Students paste their URL as it goes live — creates a real-time reveal moment. Watch for errors and troubleshoot out loud so the room learns from each fix.

Session 3 · Part 3

Demo Time

20 minutes

8 students × ~2.5 minutes each. No slides — just the live site and honest reflection.

Strict timing. Use a visible countdown timer. Tell presenters to open their live URL before they're called. Format: show the site, say one sentence about what it is, share the one prompt that changed everything.

Your 2.5 Minutes

1 "This is [site name] and it's for [who / what it's about]..."
2 "The one prompt that made the biggest difference was [show or describe it]..."
3 "Here's what broke — and what I'd try next [or how I fixed it]..."
Leave this slide up during all the presentations — it keeps presenters on track without the instructor needing to intervene. The third prompt is important: normalise that things broke. It always happens and it's always instructive.

What AI Is Good At — and What Still Needs You

AI is genuinely great at
  • Speed — a functional layout in minutes
  • Structure — sections, hierarchy, flow
  • Options — generate 3 versions and pick the best
  • Fixing itself — ask it to correct what it got wrong
What still needs you
  • Your actual story, voice, and content
  • Judgment about what looks right
  • Real photos, real testimonials, real specifics
  • Caring about the outcome
The right column is why they still have a job — say that clearly and warmly. "AI can draft everything. Only you know what's true, what sounds like you, and what actually matters to your audience."
